# The Lantern at Varrow Point — Edmund Hale
# Read up to: section 2, page 3. Do not reveal anything beyond this point.

Mira Castel arrived at Varrow Point on the last ferry of the season, with a single trunk, a black dog named Pitch, and a letter appointing her keeper of the light. The town behind her was already shuttering for winter. No one had come to see her off, and no one waited on the pier.

The lighthouse stood at the end of a stone causeway that the tide swallowed twice a day. Mira learned the timing of it within her first week: two hours of dry crossing in the morning, two in the late afternoon, and between them nothing but grey water and the cry of gulls. She kept a tide table pinned above the stove and crossed it off page by page.

The previous keeper had left in a hurry. His coat still hung on the hook by the door, and his boots stood beneath it, and the logbook — the one record every keeper was bound by law to maintain — was gone. Mira searched the lamp room, the cellar, the narrow rooms of the tower, and found only the gap on the shelf where it should have been.

The townspeople of Varrow did not speak of the old keeper. When Mira asked the grocer his name, the man only said, "He's not here now," and gave her change without meeting her eyes. She walked home along the causeway as the tide came in, and Pitch growled at the water the whole way.

On the seventh night a bell rang out over the sound. It was not the church bell — that one she knew, cracked and flat. This was deeper, and it came from the water itself, from somewhere out past the rocks where no buoy was marked on any chart. Mira stood at the gallery rail and counted: nine strokes, then silence.

In the morning she asked the grocer's wife about the bell. The woman went pale. "The Drowning Bell," she said. "It rings for them that the sea means to take. Old Tom heard it the week before he went." She would say nothing more, and Mira walked back to the light with the words turning over in her mind, and the gap on the shelf seeming larger than before.

The lamp itself was a comfort. Each dusk Mira climbed the spiral stair, wound the clockwork that turned the lens, and trimmed the wick until the flame stood clean and white. The light swept the sound in long slow arcs, and for the hours she tended it she was not afraid. It was only in the dark between sweeps that the cold got in.

She began to keep her own logbook, a school exercise book she'd brought for sketching. She wrote the weather, the tide, the hours the lamp burned. And then, because there was no one to read it, she began to write the other things too: the bell, the missing pages, the way Pitch would not go near the cellar door.

The cellar door was iron, and it was locked, and there was no key on any of the hooks. Mira put her ear to it once and heard nothing but the sea moving under the rock. Still, the dog would not pass it, and at night she sometimes thought she heard the faint sound of water dripping where no pipe ran.

It was on the ninth day that she found the first page of the old logbook, torn out and folded small, wedged behind the clock mechanism in the lamp room. The handwriting was cramped and hurried. "If you are reading this," it began, "then the light is yours now, and so is the rest of it. Do not answer the bell. Whatever it tells you, do not answer the bell." The page ended there. The rest was gone.
